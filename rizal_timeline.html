Introduction
José Rizal: The Philippines' Remarkable Hero
Dr. José Rizal, c. 1890
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/José_Rizal

When we hear the name of Dr. José Rizal, the first thing that comes to mind is that he is our national hero — a hero who fought for our country's independence not with a sword, but with a pen. His novels became the eye-openers that awakened Filipinos to fight for the freedom that had been robbed by centuries of Spanish colonization. Rizal became an unforgettable figure in Philippine history, and it is only right for the younger generation to know him better.

Through this blog post, we will look closely at the life, works, and writings of José Rizal — examining the biological factors inherited from his family, the environmental forces that shaped his consciousness, the transformative experiences he had at home and abroad, and the remarkable character that emerged from it all.

II. Biological Factors
Inherited Traits & Physical Characteristics
The genius of Rizal did not emerge in a vacuum — it was rooted deeply in his bloodline and physical constitution.

Dr. José Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda was of mixed Tagalog, Chinese, Spanish, and Japanese ancestry — a cosmopolitan genetic heritage that contributed to his unusually broad intellectual range. From his mother, Teodora Alonso, he inherited an extraordinary capacity for language and learning. Teodora was herself a woman of remarkable education: a mathematician, poet, natural scientist, and linguist in her own right. From his father, Francisco Mercado, Rizal inherited practicality, discipline, and an entrepreneurial spirit.

Physically, Rizal stood at approximately 4'11" (150 cm) — small in stature, but this only sharpened his awareness of how colonial society used physical characteristics to impose hierarchies and humiliate Filipinos. Rather than being diminished by it, he transformed that awareness into the moral indignation that powered his writing. He was also a gifted athlete: an accomplished fencer, swimmer, gymnast, and marksman who refused to allow his size to define his capabilities.

Perhaps most significantly, Rizal inherited an exceptional gift for fine motor control — excelling in sculpture, oil painting, and the delicate microsurgery that ophthalmology demands. His mother's deteriorating eyesight was a direct biological catalyst: her failing vision moved him to specialize in ophthalmology, transforming personal love into professional vocation. The decision would ultimately restore her sight.

III. Environmental Factors — Family Background
Family and Early Childhood
Rizal's character was built long before he ever set foot in a classroom. It was built at the family table.

Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, the seventh of eleven children in one of the most respected families in the province. His father Francisco managed a hacienda and was a man of education, having studied Latin and Philosophy. The household breathed an atmosphere of learning, moral seriousness, and civic pride that was unusual for the time.

His mother, Teodora Alonso, taught him to read and write at age three — an extraordinary act of maternal investment in an era when girls were rarely educated. By age five, young José had composed his first poem. She instilled in him not only literacy but a profound religious and moral sensibility. Every evening, the family prayed the Angelus together. Rizal credited her as the most formative influence on his character.

His ten siblings — Saturnina, Paciano, Narcisa, Olimpia, Lucia, Maria, Concepcion, Josefa, Trinidad, and Soledad — formed a rich social world. His brother Paciano was especially influential: deeply involved in the revolutionary circles of Father Burgos and the GomBurZa, Paciano inspired young José to think of himself as a Filipino first and a subject of Spain second.

"She taught me to read and to stammer out the first prayers. She showed me how a life could be lived with dignity even under the shadow of injustice."

— Rizal, on his mother Teodora Alonso

A pivotal early trauma came in 1869, when colonial authorities imprisoned Teodora Alonso for two and a half years on fabricated charges of attempting to poison her sister-in-law. The eight-year-old Rizal watched his mother — gentle, educated, and innocent — dragged away by the colonial system. This injustice left a wound that never fully closed, and that eventually found its expression in every page he wrote.

As a child, Rizal loved to sketch, paint, read, and write. His tutors — Maestro Celestino, Maestro Lucas Padua, and later Leon Monroy (who taught him Spanish and Latin) — recognized an unusual mind. He was naughty and playful, too. He loved to tease his sister Olimpia, grieved deeply when his little sister Concepcion died, and challenged neighbourhood children to contests of wit and nerve. He was, in short, fully human — which makes his eventual heroism all the more remarkable.

III. Environmental Factors — Education
Higher Education and the Making of a Mind
From Calamba to Madrid to Heidelberg — Rizal's education was a journey across continents and ideologies.
Ateneo Municipal de Manila, Intramuros — where Rizal earned top honors in every subject
Source: https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcTLBtFHUvHNCwEGeuq74tA0CjfDEuHa8qatyw&s

At eleven years old, Rizal passed the entrance examination for Ateneo Municipal de Manila. The college registrar initially refused his admission — he was slight, pale, and a late enrollee. It took the intervention of Father Burgos to secure his place. He enrolled not under his family surname "Mercado" (which was on bad terms with the authorities due to Paciano's associations) but as "Rizal." This early act of navigating colonial bureaucracy planted the first seed of his political consciousness.

At Ateneo, Rizal was an "Emperor" — the title given to the most academically distinguished student in class. He graduated with highest honors in all subjects, joined the Academy of Natural Sciences and the Academy of Spanish Literature, and served as an officer of the Marian Congregation. He was not merely a student; he was already an institution.

He then enrolled at the University of Santo Tomás to study Philosophy and Letters, then shifted to Medicine — driven by the desire to heal his mother's failing eyes. UST, under Dominican administration, was a different experience: openly discriminatory toward Filipino students, intellectually conservative, and dismissive of the kind of bold thinking Rizal had developed at Ateneo. He grew restless. Meanwhile, his attention was not exclusively academic; he fell in love successively with Segunda Katigbak, Leonor Valenzuela, and Leonor Rivera — the great love of his life.

In 1882, he secretly left for Spain, completing his Licentiate in Medicine and his Licenciado en Filosofía y Letras from the Universidad Central de Madrid — both with highest distinction. He then specialized in ophthalmology under Dr. Louis de Wecker in Paris and Dr. Otto Becker at the University of Heidelberg. By the time he returned to Asia, he was among the most comprehensively educated Filipinos in history.

IV. Life Experiences — Literary Works
The Works That Changed a Nation
Rizal's pen was his most powerful weapon — and the Spanish colonial government knew it.

Rizal's literary output was vast and varied. Before his landmark novels, he was already a poet of distinction. His early poems include Mi Primera Inspiracion (for his mother's birthday), Un Recuerdo de Mi Pueblo (a tribute to Calamba), and devotional works on religion and education such as Al Niño Jesus and Por La Educacion Recibe Lustre la Patria. His contest-winning poem A La Juventud Filipina — "To the Filipino Youth" — proclaimed that the young generation was the hope of the nation and remains one of the most celebrated poems in Philippine literature.

Noli Me Tángere (Touch Me Not), published in Berlin in 1887, is Rizal's most powerful literary achievement. Through the story of Crisostomo Ibarra, it exposed the brutal injustices of colonial Spanish rule — the corruption of the friar orders, the exploitation of Filipino peasants, the systematic humiliation of the "Indios." The novel was not merely protest literature; it was a mirror held up to Philippine society with devastating clarity. Its publication was an act of extraordinary moral courage.

El Filibusterismo (The Subversive), published in Ghent in 1891, was darker and more radical. Where Noli depicted the hope of peaceful reform, El Fili — through the bitter, vengeful figure of Simoun — explored what happens when that hope is extinguished. It was a warning: that a people pushed too far will seek more drastic remedies. The Spanish colonial government recognized the novel as a direct threat and moved aggressively to suppress it and destroy its author.

"I want to show to those who deny us our patriotism... that we know how to die for our duty and our convictions."

— Rizal, dedicating El Filibusterismo to the GomBurZa

IV. Life Experiences — Travels
Life Abroad: A World Education
Each country Rizal visited added a new dimension to his understanding of freedom, justice, and what the Philippines could become.
1882 — Spain
Barcelona & Madrid
Rizal arrived in Barcelona on June 16, 1882, welcomed by fellow Filipinos at Plaza de Cataluña. He wrote El Amor Patrio ("Love of Country") under the pen name "Laong Laan," published in the Diariong Tagalog. In Madrid, he completed his medical and philosophy degrees, painted, sculpted, and began writing Noli Me Tángere. He also met and fell for Consuelo Ortiga but held back out of loyalty to Leonor Rivera.
1885–1886 — France & Germany
Paris & Heidelberg — Ophthalmology
In Paris, Rizal specialized in ophthalmology under the renowned Dr. Louis de Wecker and befriended Juan Luna and Félix Resurrección Hidalgo. In Germany, he worked under Dr. Otto Becker at the University Eye Hospital in Heidelberg, mastered German, and wrote the poem A Las Flores de Heidelberg — a homesick tribute to the Philippines. In Berlin, on March 21, 1887, Noli Me Tángere was published. He dedicated it to the Philippines.
1888 — Japan
Land of the Rising Sun
Rizal was struck by Japan's cleanliness, discipline, and rapid modernization. He lived in a Spanish legation with Juan Pérez Caballero and fell in love with Seiko Usui (O Sei-San). He studied Japanese theater, martial arts, language, and culture. He eventually left Japan — and O Sei-San — for his country and his mission.
1888 — United States
Democracy and Its Contradictions
Traveling across the United States, Rizal was impressed by the high standard of living but troubled by its contradictions: racial discrimination against Chinese and Japanese workers, the bias of the justice system toward white Americans, and the worship of money over human dignity. America showed him what democracy looked like — and also what it failed to deliver for the marginalized.
1888–1889 — London
Reclaiming Philippine History
In London, Rizal studied at the British Museum and annotated Antonio de Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas — reclaiming pre-colonial Philippine history from Spanish distortion. He met Dr. Reinhold Rost, who inspired him to write for the Trübner's Records journal. He also founded the Kidlat Club and the Indios Bravos, and wrote prolifically for La Solidaridad.
1890–1891 — Belgium
El Filibusterismo & Personal Crisis
In Belgium, Rizal faced financial hardship and distressing news from home — the Dominican friars had imposed punishing rents on the Calamba hacienda, effectively driving his family off their land. Heartbroken, he wrote the poem A Mi Musa. Despite everything, he completed El Filibusterismo on March 29, 1891, and published it in Ghent. He also met Nellie Boustead, another romantic interest who ultimately did not convert to his faith — ending their relationship.

IV. Life Experiences — Exile, Trial & Death
Dapitan, Trial, and the Final Act
In exile, Rizal's heroism found its most tangible, daily, human expression. In death, it became eternal.
Rizal during his exile in Dapitan, Zamboanga del Norte (1892–1896)
Source: https://mb.com.ph/2023/12/29/rizal-s-life-in-exile

In 1892, Rizal returned to Manila and founded La Liga Filipina, a civic organization advocating unity, mutual aid, and peaceful reform. Within days of its first meeting, he was arrested and deported to Dapitan, a remote town in Zamboanga del Norte — the Spanish colonial government's answer to ideas it could not refute.

But exile in Dapitan revealed Rizal's deepest character. Far from being silenced, he built a school for local children — teaching them for free. He engineered an aqueduct to supply clean water to the community. He operated a free medical clinic and treated thousands of patients, many of whom had never seen a doctor. He conducted botanical and zoological field research that was formally recognized by scientific societies in Europe, resulting in the discovery of several new species. He also fell in love with Josephine Bracken, an Irish-Filipino woman who became his partner, and established a model farm and household. Dapitan was the living proof that Rizal's ideals were not merely literary — they were practical and daily.

When the Katipunan revolution broke out in August 1896, Rizal was already on a ship bound for Cuba — having been granted permission to serve as a military physician for Spain. He was arrested in Barcelona, returned to Manila in chains, and imprisoned in Fort Santiago. He was accused of being the mastermind of the revolution he had neither planned nor approved.

"I die without seeing the dawn brighten over my native land. You, who will see it, greet it for me — do not forget those who fell during the darksome night."

— José Rizal, Mi Último Adiós (My Last Farewell), December 29, 1896

On December 26, 1896, a military court-martial found him guilty of rebellion, sedition, and illegal association — based almost entirely on his published novels. He was denied a meaningful defense. He was sentenced to death. The night before his execution, he secretly wrote his final and greatest poem, Mi Último Adiós, and concealed it in a lamp he gave to his family.

The execution of Dr. José Rizal at Bagumbayan, December 30, 1896

At 7:03 a.m. on December 30, 1896, José Rizal was executed by firing squad at Bagumbayan (now Luneta/Rizal Park), Manila. He was 35 years old. Witnesses reported that he attempted to turn to face the squad as the volley rang out — and that as he fell, his final word was "Consummatum est" — "It is finished." The authorities shot him once more in the head to be certain.

The Spaniards believed his death would end the revolution. Instead, it became its greatest fuel. Within two years, the Malolos Congress had established the first Philippine Republic. Rizal had not lived to see it — but without him, it would not have existed.

V. Character Analysis
Strengths, Weaknesses, and the Evolution of a Hero
Rizal was fully human — which is precisely what makes his heroism so extraordinary.

Rizal's character was neither saintly nor simple. He was a man of immense gifts and genuine vulnerabilities — and understanding both is essential to understanding why his life matters.

Virtues & Strengths
Extraordinary intellectual breadth — mastered medicine, law, literature, art, sculpture, and natural science simultaneously
Moral courage: published works exposing colonial abuses at great personal risk
Deep compassion: treated patients for free, built community infrastructure in Dapitan
Iron discipline: produced landmark novels, scientific papers, and thousands of letters while practicing medicine full-time
Patriotism without hatred: sought reform through education and persuasion, not violence or vengeance
Profound dignity in suffering: faced exile and execution with equanimity and continued creative productivity
Shortcomings & Weaknesses
Political naïveté: believed peaceful colonial reform was achievable, underestimating Spanish intransigence
Indecisiveness during the Revolution: his ambivalence left the Katipunan without unified moral leadership
Complex romantic life: relationships with Leonor Rivera, Nelly Boustead, O Sei-San caused personal turmoil
Ilustrado elitism: early writings sometimes overlooked the immediate material needs of the poor majority
Overconfidence that literature alone could reshape a colonial society entrenched over three centuries
Evolution of Character Over Time
Childhood (1861–1872): Brilliant but sheltered — shaped by family values and first encounters with colonial injustice that ignited his moral sensibility
Student & European years (1872–1892): Became radicalized intellectually; developed his reformist philosophy; wrote his major works; emerged as a cosmopolitan thinker
Return & Dapitan exile (1887–1896): Grew increasingly pragmatic and humanitarian; shifted from abstract reformism to direct daily service; proved his ideals through lived action
Final months (1896): Achieved spiritual maturity — accepted martyrdom with deliberate, peaceful defiance; his death transformed him into the enduring conscience of the nation

Rizal's legacy today is not merely that of a man who died for his country. It is the legacy of a complete human being who lived fully — who loved, doubted, grieved, laughed, and created — and who chose, in the end, to face death rather than betray what he believed. In that choice lies the deepest lesson of his life: that integrity, carried to its conclusion, is its own form of heroism.

"He who does not know how to look back at where he came from will not reach his destination."

— Dr. José Rizal
